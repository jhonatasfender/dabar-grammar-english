## Scale (roughly, context can override)

- **must** — strong inference from premises: _The logs show root access — it **must be** an insider._
- **should / ought to** — expectation from normal course: _He **should be** home by now._
- **may / might / could** — compatible possibilities; **might** often sounds **more cautious** than **may** in contemporary English.
- **can’t** — logical / empirical incompatibility: _That **can’t be** the stable build — the hash differs._

## _Could_ vs _can_ (epistemic)

Epistemic **could** is common for **open possibility**; **can** in epistemic assertions is **narrower** and sometimes **less idiomatic** in affirmatives (_It can be true_ is limited contexts). **Can’t** for negative epistemic is **fully productive**.

## Negation traps

- **must not** is often **deontic** (“not allowed”), not “unlikely”. For low probability prefer **can’t** or **is unlikely to**.

## Academic caution

Pair modals with **visible evidence** to avoid sounding arbitrary: _This **may** reflect selection bias, given …_

## Quick checklist

1. Are you marking **degree of belief**, not **desire** or **obligation**?
2. Does **must** have **supporting premises** in the discourse?
3. Is **can’t** truly **incompatibility**, not mere unlikelihood?
