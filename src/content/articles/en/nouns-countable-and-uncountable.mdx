## What is really at stake (beyond the textbook split)

**Countability** does not mirror the physical world; it reflects **whether the grammar lets you count instances** with _one/two/many_ + bare plural (no partitive). If the language instead wants _much/little/a great deal of_ or a **partitive** (_a piece of_, _items of_), you are in a **mass** or **non-count** reading.

That is why _coffee_ can be mass (_I drink coffee_) and count (_two coffees_ = two servings): **two construals** of one lexeme, not “two different nouns”.

## Count nouns: quick diagnostics

- **Plural in generic statements:** _Hypotheses vary in strength._
- **a/an** + singular: _We propose **a** hierarchical model._
- **Bare numerals:** _three cohorts_, _twenty participants_ — not ✱*twenty evidences*.

If plural “sounds odd” even when your L1 feels plural, suspect **uncountable English** and reach for a partitive or a different head noun.

## Typical uncountables (why advanced writers still slip)

In standard English, these groups are usually **mass / non-plural** (no regular plural):

- **Academic abstractions:** _research_, _evidence_, _information_, _feedback_, _progress_, _work_ (labour/output reading).
- **Aggregate non-items:** _furniture_, _equipment_, _luggage_, _software_ (often mass), _staff_ (collective).

Classic learner errors: ✱**informations**, ✱**advices**, ✱**furnitures**, ✱**researches** (for “research activity” or “studies”). Natural repairs:

- _**pieces of** information_, _**items of** evidence_, _**two** studies_, _**three** experiments_, _**pieces of** furniture_, _**staff** members_.

## Partitives and implicit “classifiers”

English is not classifier languages like Mandarin, but **partitives** do similar work:

- _a **body of** literature_, _a **set of** constraints_, _two **pieces of** evidence_, _a **range of** outcomes_.

In methods and results, prefer **count heads for events** where you can: _**three** trials_, not ✱*three researches* — use _three **studies**_ / _three **surveys**_.

## Dual-life stems (mass vs count reading)

| Stem    | Mass / non-partitioned             | Count / instance or type                         |
| ------- | ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| _paper_ | _The printer is out of **paper**._ | _She published **two papers**._                  |
| _time_  | _We need **more time**._           | _I called him **three times**._                  |
| _glass_ | _The window is made of **glass**._ | _She broke **two glasses**._                     |
| _iron_  | _The gate is made of **iron**._    | _Use **two irons** for the shirts._ (tool sense) |

**Type plurals** (special count use): _Italian **wines**_, _difficult **times**_, _**steels** of different grades_ — plural marks **categories**, not “pieces” of one mass.

## _Research_, _data_, and house style

- **_Research_** as mass: _**Much** research suggests…_, _**little** prior research_. To count projects, use _studies_, _papers_, _projects_, _experiments_.
- **_Data_:** _data **is**_ (set/mass treatment) vs _data **are**_ (plural of _datum_, still required in some journals). **Pick one convention** per manuscript; mixing within a section annoys reviewers.

## Collectives: singular or plural?

_The committee **has** decided_ (body as unit) vs _The committee **are** divided_ (focus on members) — more natural in **British English**. For many US journals, **singular** agreement is the safer default unless you explicitly foreground individuals.

## _Number_ vs _amount_ (why reviewers stet this)

- _**The number of** errors **is** small._ — _number_ + **count** plural noun.
- _**The amount of** noise **was** high._ — _amount_ + **mass**.

✱**amount of participants** is strongly non-idiomatic; use _**number of** participants_.

## L1 interference (Portuguese / Spanish sketch)

- **“Do research”** calques → _**conduct** research_ / _**carry out** a **study**_ — not ✱*make a research*.
- Definite article in L1 generics does not map 1:1: _Students need sleep_ (zero article) vs _**The** students in cohort B_ (delimited set). Pair with [Articles: _the_ or no article](/en/articles-definite-and-zero).

## Pre-submission checklist

1. For each abstract noun: is plural licensed, or do you need a **partitive** / different head?
2. Do _much/many_, _little/few_, _a large **number** of_ / _a large **amount** of_ match the head?
3. Any L1 plural calques (_advices_, _informations_)?
4. One consistent treatment for _data_ and _research_ in the file?

## Five-minute → ten-minute practice

In one paragraph (intro or methods), underline every NP with an abstract head. For each: (a) mass vs count in English; (b) if mass, rewrite with a **partitive** or **count paraphrase** (_study_, _trial_, _dataset_); (c) check _number/amount_.
