## How to use this hub

If you are reading for **exams**, start with the contrasts that generate the most errors: **present perfect vs simple past**, **past perfect vs simple past**, and **will vs going to**. If you are reading for **writing**, add **progressive vs simple** for narrative pacing and **perfect progressive** for duration up to a point.

If **do / did / will / would / have / had** and **modals** feel like an undifferentiated blur, read [Auxiliaries and modals — overview](/en/auxiliaries-and-modals-overview) first — it maps each helper to its job before you tackle the contrasts below.

## Simple vs progressive

- **Simple** forms often present a situation as **whole** or **habitual**: _She works remotely._
- **Progressive** forms highlight **ongoing** or **temporary** situations: _She is working on a grant proposal._

Progressives are not automatic with “now”; some verbs (often mental states) prefer simple forms in many contexts: _I understand_ (not usually _I am understanding_).

## Past continuous vs simple past (narrative aspect)

In past-time storytelling, **simple past** usually carries the **mainline events**; **past progressive** stages **ongoing background** or **overlap**: _I **was rerunning** the pipeline when the scheduler **killed** the job._ The contrast is **viewpoint**, not “long” vs “short” in the abstract. For a dedicated map, see [Past continuous and past simple](/en/past-continuous-and-past-simple).

## Present perfect vs simple past

Use the contrast as a **viewpoint** problem:

- **Simple past** anchors an event in a **finished** past frame: _I submitted the paper on Tuesday._
- **Present perfect** ties a past situation to **now** (relevance, open time frame, or life experience): _I have submitted the revised version._

If you name a **closed** past time (_yesterday_, _in 2019_), **simple past** is usually the default.

## Past perfect: “before another past”

**Past perfect** marks **earlier-than** within a past narrative: _She realized she had left the references off the PDF._

Do not sprinkle past perfect without a clear **later past** anchor.

## Future: will, going to, present progressive

- **will**: spontaneous decision, offers/promises, strong prediction: _I’ll email you the draft._
- **going to**: plan/intention with prior thought; evidence-based prediction: _It’s going to rain._
- **present progressive for future**: fixed arrangement: _I’m meeting the supervisor at 9._

## Study drill (5 minutes)

Take one paragraph of your own writing and **justify every finite verb** in two words: “finished past”, “open relevance”, “background”, “temporary”, etc. If you cannot justify a perfect, try removing it and see whether meaning collapses.
