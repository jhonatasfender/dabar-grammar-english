## Cooperative principle and maxims

Grice proposed that talk exchanges are governed by a **Cooperative Principle**: make your contribution appropriate to the shared purpose of the conversation. He unpacked this via **maxims** (Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner).

**Conversational implicatures** are inferences the hearer draws when an utterance appears to **flout** or **violate** a maxim while the speaker is still assumed to be cooperative.

## Scalar implicature

A speaker says _Some students passed_.

If _some_ is compatible with _all_ in logical terms, why do many hearers infer _not all_? Scalar reasoning ties **informativity** to the Quantity maxim: a stronger term on the scale (_all_) would have been more informative if true.

## Advanced angle for literature students

In **literary** discourse, apparent maxim violations may signal **irony**, **politeness**, or **unreliable narration**. Your analysis should separate:

1. **What is said** (truth-conditional content, however theorised).
2. **What is implicated** (recoverable via reasoning about the speaker’s intentions and norms).

## Practice prompt

Take a short dialogue from a novel or play. Mark an utterance that is **literally true** but **pragmatically loaded**. Write two paragraphs: one defending a **Gricean** story, one noting limitations (e.g., **exhaustivity**, **generalised** vs **particularised** implicature).
