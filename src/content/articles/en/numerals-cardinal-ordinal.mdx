## Cardinals vs ordinals

- **Cardinal:** _three experiments_, _chapter 5_, _room 101_ (labels often stay cardinal in technical prose).
- **Ordinal:** _the third experiment_, _the fifth chapter_, _his twenty-first visit_.

After a noun modified by an ordinal, a **definite** article is normal: _the **first** author_, not ✱*a first author* (unless you mean “one among several first authors”, which is rare).

### Compounds 21–99 (ordinals)

Use **cardinal tens + hyphen + ordinal unit**: _twenty-**first** (21st)_, _thirty-**second** (32nd)_, _ninety-**ninth** (99th)_.

### Larger round numbers

| Cardinal (typical forms) | Ordinal              |
| ------------------------ | -------------------- |
| one hundred; a hundred   | hundredth (100th)    |
| one thousand; a thousand | thousandth (1,000th) |
| one million; a million   | millionth            |
| one billion; a billion   | billionth            |

## Hyphens in compounds

In **21–99** (when not round tens), English hyphenates: _twenty-**one**_, _ninety-**nine**_. Round tens are open: _twenty_, _ninety_. Larger compounds follow the same logic: _one hundred twenty-**three**_ (AmE) / _one hundred **and** twenty-**three**_ (BrE, often with _and_ before the final tens–units chunk).

## _And_ with thousands and decimals

- **Thousands:** BrE often inserts _and_ after hundreds in speech and careful writing: _two thousand **and** five_. AmE frequently omits it: _two thousand five_. Both are acceptable in their varieties; **consistency** within a document matters.
- **Decimals:** Read the point as _point_: _3.14_ → _three point one four_ (not ✱*three comma fourteen*). In running text, a comma as decimal separator is **not** standard English typography—use a **full stop**.

## Fractions and proportions

- **Simple fractions:** _a/one half_, _a third_, _two fifths_ — note plural agreement on the denominator when the numerator is not 1.
- **More formal:** _three **in** ten participants_ or _a **proportion** of 0.42_ may suit academic prose better than long spoken fractions.

## Dates and labels

- **US-style:** _May 5_ / _May 5th_ — often read “May fifth”.
- **UK-style:** _5 May_ / _the 5th of May_ — “the fifth of May”.

Do not mix ordinal suffixes into ISO-style numeric dates in body text unless your style guide requires it.

## _A_ vs _one_ with count nouns

- _**A** hundred participants_ — typical indefinite packaging.
- _**One** hundred participants_ — stresses the round number or contrasts with another count.

See also article choice with quantity in your variety’s conventions for _a million_ vs _one million_ (both occur; tone and contrast drive the choice).

## Five-minute practice

Write out in words: (1) your year of birth as an ordinal age (_in her **thirty-second** year_); (2) a lab temperature _36.5_; (3) a sample size _1,024_. Check hyphens, _and_, and whether each context wants a **cardinal** or **ordinal**.
