## The comma splice (and how to fix it)

A **comma splice** joins two independent clauses with only a comma: _\*The results are stable, they replicate across cohorts._

Fixes (choose one):

- **period** (often best): _The results are stable. They replicate across cohorts._
- **coordinator**: _The results are stable, **and** they replicate…_
- **semicolon** (close relation): _The results are stable; they replicate…_
- **subordination**: _Because the results are stable, they replicate…_

## Semicolon vs colon

- **Semicolon** links **closely related** independent clauses: _The effect is small; nonetheless, it is robust._
- **Colon** introduces **explanation, lists, or appositives**: _We focus on one question: whether training size saturates._

Do not put a colon after a fragment that cannot stand as a lead-in.

## Relative clauses: restrictive vs non-restrictive

- **Restrictive** (identifies which one): **no commas**: _Participants who completed both sessions were retained._
- **Non-restrictive** (adds extra info): **commas**: _The preprint, which we revised twice, is now online._

A common advanced error is **over-comma-ing** restrictive relatives, which blurs identification.

## Lists and parallel structure

In enumerated lists, keep **parallel grammar**: if you start with verbs, continue with verbs. Punctuation before the final item follows your chosen convention (Oxford comma or not)—**be consistent** within a document.

## Two-minute diagnostic

Take any long sentence (>35 words). Can you name its **main clause**? If not, split. If yes, check whether commas mark **true pauses** or mask **splice errors**.
