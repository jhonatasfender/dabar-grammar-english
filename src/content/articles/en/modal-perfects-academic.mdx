## Overview

Modal perfect constructions—**must have V-en**, **might have V-en**, **should have V-en**, and related forms—are frequent in advanced academic registers. They package **aspect** (perfect) with **modality** (epistemic or deontic), often marking inference, reproach, or hypothetical reasoning.

## Epistemic vs. deontic

In **epistemic** readings, the speaker evaluates the likelihood of a past situation relative to evidence now available:

> The results **may have been** affected by sampling bias.

**Deontic** or **evaluative** readings concern obligation or appropriateness relative to a norm:

> Reviewers **should have flagged** the confound earlier in the process.

Graduate readers should learn to distinguish these on the basis of **truth-conditional** paraphrase and **co-text** (verbs of evidence, policy, or recommendation).

## Perfect + modal scope

The perfect places the **situation time** before a reference time anchored by the modal. In research articles, this reference is often **now** (the moment of writing) or the **narrated experiment timeline**. Mis-scoping produces awkward or ambiguous sentences—compare:

- _The participants **could have completed** the task before the interruption._ (past possibility)
- _The participants **could complete** the task before the interruption._ (future-oriented ability / root modality)

## Pedagogical note

For **Letters** students, map each construction to **speech act** function in your discipline’s genre conventions (methods, discussion, limitations). Avoid treating modals as interchangeable politeness markers without semantic motivation.
