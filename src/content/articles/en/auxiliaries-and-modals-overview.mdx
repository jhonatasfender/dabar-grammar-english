## Why this feels messy

In many languages a single finite verb carries tense and polarity. In English, **tense and auxiliary choice** often sit on **separate words**: _I **have** **been** **doing** it._ Learners then ask whether _do_, _did_, _will_, _would_, _have_, _had_, or _could_ “is the verb”. The useful split is: **auxiliary / modal** (grammar scaffolding) vs **lexical verb** (the action or state you mean).

Below, **one lexical verb** (_do_ = “perform/carry out”) illustrates how **each helper** changes the message. With **other** main verbs (_work_, _study_), **do** usually appears only as **do-support** (questions, negation, emphasis) — not as the action word.

| --------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **I do**              | Eu faço                               |
| **I did**             | Eu fiz                                |
| **I don’t do**        | Eu não faço                           |
| **I didn’t do**       | Eu não fiz                            |
| **I will do**         | Eu farei                              |
| **I would do**        | Eu faria                              |
| **I have been doing** | Eu tenho feito (em curso / até agora) |
| **I had done**        | Eu tinha feito                        |

## _Do_ / _does_ / _did_: two different jobs

- **Lexical _do_** — the main verb: _I **do** the invoicing on Fridays._
- **Do-support** — when there is no other auxiliary, **do** carries tense and negation for simple present/past: _**Do** you agree?_ / _She **doesn’t** agree._ / _**Did** they leave?_  
  This is why **don’t** / **doesn’t** / **didn’t** pair with the **plain** infinitive: _He doesn’t **know**_, not ✱*doesn’t knows*.

For inversion in questions and full treatment of embedded clauses, see [Question forms](/en/question-forms).

## _Will_ and _would_: future vs layered “distance”

- **_Will_** — default **future** marker and spontaneous decision: _I **will** send the draft tonight._ (Contrast with _going to_ and arrangements in [Future forms](/en/future-forms-will-going-to-present-continuous).)
- **_Would_** — **past of _will_** in reported speech; **hypothetical** or **polite** softening in present-time requests; and **past habit** in narrative alongside _used to_: _As a child, he **would** walk to school._ See [Past habits: _used to_, _would_, past simple](/en/past-habits-used-to-would-past-simple).

## _Have_ / _has_ / _had_: perfect aspect

**Perfect** forms tie an event or state to **another time** (often **now** or a **later past**):

- **_Have/has + past participle_** — present perfect: [Present perfect](/en/present-perfect).
- **_Had + past participle_** — “before another past”: [Past perfect](/en/past-perfect).
- With **_been + -ing_** you add **duration or process** up to that anchor: [Present perfect — simple and continuous](/en/present-perfect-simple-and-continuous).

## _Be_ + _-ing_: progressive aspect

**Am / is / are / was / were + -ing** marks **ongoing**, **temporary**, or **background** situations — not a “modal”, but the other common auxiliary stack: _She **is working** remotely this month._ For copular _be_ (identity, location), see [Present simple — _to be_](/en/present-simple-be). Broader contrasts: [Verb tenses in real use](/en/verb-tenses-in-use).

## Modal verbs (_can_, _could_, _must_, _should_, …)

**Modals** (and close semi-modals like **_have to_**, **_need to_**) add **ability, permission, obligation, deduction, or tentativeness**. They are **finite** but usually **no _to_** before the next verb: _You **must** **submit** by noon._

- Permission, obligation, **must** vs **have to** vs **need to**: [Modals for permission, obligation, and prohibition](/en/modals-permission-and-obligation).
- **Can/could** for **past ability**: [Past ability](/en/past-ability).

## Five-minute practice

Pick **one** paragraph of English you wrote. For each finite verb group, label: **(1)** first auxiliary if any, **(2)** main verb, **(3)** time/aspect (simple / progressive / perfect / modal). If you see **do** before another verb, say whether it is **do-support** or **lexical _do_**.
