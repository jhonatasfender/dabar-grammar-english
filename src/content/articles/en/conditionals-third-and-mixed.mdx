## Third conditional (past + past)

- **Form (canonical):** _If + past perfect_, **would have + past participle** (plus _could/might have_ for narrower modality).
- **Meaning:** both **P** and **Q** are **counterfactual in the past** — the speaker treats the situation as closed and non-actual.

_If we had replicated the study, we would have spotted the confound earlier._

## Mixed conditionals (why they exist)

Speakers often need a **past cause** with a **present or open consequent** (or the mirror image):

- _If you **had listened**, you **wouldn’t be** in this mess._ (past unreal **if** → present result)
- _If I **were** braver, I **would have spoken** up yesterday._ (present character state → past unreal outcome — less frequent, stylistically marked)

The guiding question is **which time sphere** each clause **truth-conditionally** targets, not memorised “types”.

## Modal variation

**Would** is the default hypothetical consequent; **could/might** add **ability** or **epistemic openness**:

_If she had applied, she **might have been** shortlisted._

## Advanced pitfalls

1. **Tense harmony:** avoid a simple past in the **if**-clause for a clearly counterfactual past antecedent — prefer **past perfect**.
2. **Over-explicitness:** in coordinated structures, ellipsis should remain **parallel** (_If A had … and **(if)** B had …_).
3. **Register:** _If it were not for …_ / _Had we known …_ (inversion) are common in formal prose; keep subject–auxiliary order consistent after inversion.

## Quick checklist

1. Is the **antecedent** about a **closed non-actual past**?
2. Does the **consequent** match the **result time** you intend (past vs present vs open)?
3. Does the modal mark **result** (would), **ability** (could), or **narrow possibility** (might)?
