## Zero conditional (general / habitual)

- **Typical form:** present (or modal present) in both clauses — not “timeless” by magic, but **generic** or **habitual**.
- **Function:** package **law-like**, **procedural**, or **standing** relations.

_If the red LED flashes, the sensor is misaligned._

Avoid overusing zero for **one-off** future predictions — that is usually **first conditional** territory.

## First conditional (open future / real possibility)

- **Common form:** **present** (or present progressive / present perfect) in the **if**-clause, **will** or other modal in the main clause.
- **Function:** **P** is treated as **open**; **Q** is a **likely or intended** outcome.

_If the reviewers push back, we’ll revise the limitations paragraph._

**Unless** = “if not”, but polarity and **scalar implicature** differ from plain negated **if**.

## Second conditional (lower likelihood / present unreal)

- **Canonical form:** **past** in the **if**-clause, **would + infinitive** in the main clause.
- **Readings:** (i) **remote** but still conceivable future; (ii) **present counterfactual** state.

_If I had an extra month, I would rerun the analysis._

**Were** for **be** in formal registers: _If this **were** true, …_

## Modality instead of _will_

_Can, may, might, should_ in the consequent narrow the **speech act**: permission, epistemic caution, or deontic recommendation.

## Quick checklist

1. Is **P** a **standing generalisation**, an **open possibility**, or a **counterfactual world**?
2. Does the consequent modal mark **prediction**, **policy**, or **epistemic stance**?
3. Is **second** being used for **face-saving** advice (_If I were you, …_)?
