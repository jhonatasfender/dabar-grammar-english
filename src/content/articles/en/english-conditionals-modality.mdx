## Conditionals as “if P, (then) Q”

Think in **relations**, not labels:

- **Zero** (general truth): _If water freezes, it expands._
- **First** (likely future open): _If the reviewers ask for data, we’ll upload the appendix._
- **Second** (less likely / present unreal): _If I had more time, I’d rewrite the introduction._
- **Third** (past unreal): _If we had preregistered, we would have avoided that criticism._
- **Mixed** (cross-time): _If we had coded it differently, the results would be easier to explain._

## “Would” is not a tense machine

**Would** appears in polite requests, past habits, reported future-in-the-past, and hypotheticals. Use diagnostics: replace with **could/might** if ability/possibility is the point; keep **would** for the default hypothetical consequent.

## Modals: must vs have to (roughly)

- **must** often signals **speaker judgment** or **strong requirement** from the speaker’s perspective: _You must avoid circular definitions._
- **have to** often signals **external** obligation: _We have to follow the journal template._

Real usage overlaps; your discipline’s style guide may flatten the distinction.

## may, might, could (epistemic)

Epistemic readings express **confidence**:

- **might/may**: cautious inference: _This might be an artifact of preprocessing._
- **could**: open possibility (also ability): _It could also reflect sampling bias._

In academic prose, pair modals with **evidence cues** (_suggests_, _is consistent with_) to avoid sounding vague.

## Quick revision checklist

1. Is the **if-clause** about a real open future or a counterfactual world?
2. Are tense forms **backshifted** consistently in unreal scenarios?
3. Does the modal mark **obligation**, **permission**, **ability**, or **epistemic stance**?
