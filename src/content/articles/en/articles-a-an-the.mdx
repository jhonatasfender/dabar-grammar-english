## _A_ vs _an_: next **sound**, not next letter

The rule is **phonological** (the sound at the start of the very next stressed syllable), not spelling.

- Consonant sound → _a_: _a university_, _a European policy_, _a one-off experiment_, _a URL_ (if read “you-are-el”).
- Vowel sound → _an_: _an hour_, _an honest mistake_, _an X-ray_, _an NLP pipeline_ (if “en-el-pee” starts with a vowel).

Abbreviations and acronyms follow the **pronunciation** you use, not the alphabet.

## What “definite” and “indefinite” actually do

In descriptive grammar, _the_ marks **identifiability** for the target reader: the NP is recoverable from prior mention, from **modification** that makes it unique in the frame, from **shared knowledge** (_the Prime Minister_ in the country under discussion), or from **“obvious” uniqueness** in the world model (_the Sun_).

_A/an_ typically mark **non-identifiability** or **first instantiation** of a singular countable token: the reader has not yet pinned down a particular instance.

> The useful revision question is not “specific vs vague”, but: **does the reader already know _which_ instance?** If yes, _the_ tends to win; if not, _a/an_ (or zero with plural/mass—see the sister article).

## Indefinite: instance, type, or role

- **Token introduction (first mention):** _We trained **a** transformer-based classifier on the corpus._ — no particular shared classifier yet; a discourse “slot” opens.
- **Type / function / role:** _She works as **a** reviewer for two journals._ — not “one reviewer among all reviewers in the world”, but **an instance of the role**.
- **Predicative classification:** _He is **a** linguist._ — class membership; _He is **the** linguist who refuted the claim._ — individuation by restriction.

## Discourse chains: anaphora, cataphora, and bridging

- **Classic anaphora:** _We deployed **a** parser. **The** parser failed on long sentences._ — _the_ picks up the same token.
- **Cataphora:** _**The** claim advanced below rests on two lemmas._ — identifiable content comes **after** (_below_, relative clause, appositive).
- **Bridging inference:** _She bought **a** car. **The** dealer offered a warranty._ — “dealer” was not mentioned, but readers **infer** the link via “car”. Requires strong coherence; in dry prose it can feel telegraphic.

These chains intersect with [Pronouns and reference](/en/pronouns-and-reference): articles and pronouns compete in the same **information economy**.

## Definite article: restriction and **local** uniqueness

_The_ appears when a modifier **delimits** the set to a referent (or a salient set):

- _**The** students **who** passed the quiz_ vs students in general (often zero article in the generic plural—see below).
- Superlatives and ordering: _**the** best result_, _**the** first author_.
- Situationally obvious: _Close **the** door._ — unique in the immediate context.

## Generics: _a/an_, _the_ + singular, and where this article stops

Three common patterns (register-sensitive):

- **_A/an_ + singular:** _**A** tiger can weigh 300 kg._ — **typical exemplar** of the class.
- **_The_ + singular:** _**The** tiger is endangered._ — **species**-level reading (often more formal than “Tigers are…”).
- **Bare plural or bare mass:** default **generic** patterns—covered in [Articles: _the_ or no article](/en/articles-definite-and-zero) and [Countable and uncountable nouns](/en/nouns-countable-and-uncountable).

If the head is mass or generic plural, do not force _a/an_ where English wants zero.

## _A_ vs _one_: cardinality and contrast

- _We ran **one** experiment, not two._ — **numeric** focus.
- _We ran **an** experiment using adversarial examples._ — **one** among possible instances, without stressing the count.

With _only_, contrast favours _one_: _only **one** model converged_.

## Coordination and a single determiner

- Stereotyped pair (one set): _**a** knife and fork_ — “single pair” reading is common.
- Two independent items: _**a** knife and **a** spoon_ — avoids ambiguity (not a fixed kit).
- Mixed roles: _**a** teacher and mentor_ may be one person with two roles; _**a** teacher and **a** mentor_ are two referents.

## Academic prose: three-step topic progression

A robust Methods/Results pattern:

1. _**A** novel scoring function is proposed._
2. _**The** proposed scoring function combines …_
3. _**The** function was evaluated on …_

After **specification** (_proposed_, _above_, _in Section 3_), the reader can tell **which** function: _the_ is licensed. For wider cohesion patterns, see [Grammar moves for academic and professional English](/en/academic-writing-grammar).

## Degree inside the NP: _such a_ and _quite a_

Patterns like _**such a** nuisance_, _**quite a** breakthrough_ package degree in the NP; collocational frames differ from _so + adjective_. See [Intensifiers _so_ and _such_ — degree clauses and NP packaging](/en/intensifiers-so-and-such).

## Proper nouns (what is truly idiosyncratic)

Hydronyms, mountain ranges, compound state names, media outlets, and institutional names follow **lexicalised** conventions (_the Atlantic_, _the Alps_, _the BBC_, _Lake Geneva_, _Harvard University_). There is no reliable short algorithm: **corpus, dictionary, or official site** beats L1 intuition.

## Ten-minute practice

1. In one paragraph of your draft, number each singular countable NP and label **first mention**, **anaphoric retrieval**, **cataphoric**, **generic**, or **unique in frame**.
2. For each _the_, ask: **what cue** (text, world, modifier) makes the referent identifiable?
3. For each zero article on plural/mass NPs, check you are not forcing a **particularised** reading that would need _the_.
