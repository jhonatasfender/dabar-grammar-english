## What the form is really doing

- **Gnomic / general truth:** _Water boils at 100 °C._ — validity is not tied to a single episode.
- **Habitual frame:** _She commutes by train._ — repetition is implied; adverbs like _usually_, _often_ reinforce it.
- \*\*Scheduled future (timetable sense):\_ _The train leaves at 6:12._ — institutional schedule, not personal intention.
- **Performative / ceremonial:** _I accept the nomination._ — the utterance counts as the act.

## Why you cannot say _I am knowing_

Many **stative verbs** (_know_, _believe_, _want_, _own_, _resemble_) resist progressive construal because the progressive highlights a bounded process; a state is not “in progress” in the same way. Advanced learners still over-extend _-ing_ from L1 calques—check each verb class before defaulting to progressive.

## Present simple in subordinate clauses with future reference

After _when_, _as soon as_, _before_, _until_, the **simple present** often anchors a **future** situation: _When the results arrive, we will update the appendix._ Not ✱*when the results will arrive* in standard formal English.

## Advanced: historical present

In narrative, present simple can **dramatize** past events: _In 2019 the team discovers a confound._ This is stylistic, not a tense mistake—common in abstracts, blurbs, and spoken storytelling.

## Five-minute practice

Take five sentences from your own writing that use _will_ or progressive. Ask: is the situation **stable, repeated, or scheduled**? If yes, try a simple present and check whether the time adverbs still license it.
