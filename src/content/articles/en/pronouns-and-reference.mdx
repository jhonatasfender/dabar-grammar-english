## What “pronoun” means in English grammar

**Pronouns** (and **pro-forms** more broadly) **stand in for** or **point back to** material elsewhere: _She_ = the woman already in play; _it_ = the claim just made. Good academic style still demands **clear antecedents**—reviewers flag “this/which/it” when two competing referents are equally salient.

## Personal pronouns: subject vs object case

| Subject       | Object         |
| ------------- | -------------- |
| I             | me             |
| he / she / it | him / her / it |
| we            | us             |
| they          | them           |

After **prepositions** and as **non-subject** arguments, object forms are standard: _between **you and me**_, _They invited **her**_, _The effect on **them** was small._ Hypercorrection ✱**between you and I** still appears in careful speech; in edited English, stick to **me**.

**Coordination:** _She and **I** agree_ (subject) vs _The supervisor met with **her and me**_ (object)—case is determined by the **function of the whole coordination** in the clause.

### _He_, _she_, and _it_: who refers to what (and why _it_ trips people up)

**_He_** and **_she_** pick up **people** and, by convention, **named pets** or clearly personified animals: _**She** runs the lab_, _**He** revised the section_, _I saw **Max**; **he** was asleep._ Unnamed animals in the discourse are usually **_it_**: _A mouse entered; **it** disappeared under the bench._

**_It_** is the third-person pronoun for everything **outside** the _he/she_ pair: **things**, **institutions** as single entities, **ideas**, **processes**, **claims**, and sometimes **whole stretches of argument**. That is why _it_ is everywhere in academic prose—and why editors insist on a **clear antecedent** (as noted above when _this/which/it_ compete).

Two mental buckets help:

1. **Anaphoric _it_** — a **retrievable antecedent** (noun, idea, or prior span): _The algorithm stalled; **it** had hit a ceiling._ _Pluralism is attractive, but **it** raises coordination issues._

2. **Structural (“dummy”) / extraposed _it_** — **not** a substitute for a noun in the same clause; it fills the **obligatory subject slot** while the real content follows (infinitive, _that_-clause, etc.): _**It** is crucial **to replicate** the study._ _**It** remains unclear **whether** the effect generalizes._ Here _it_ does not “mean” a thing by itself—it is a **grammatical placeholder** (contrast languages that allow subjectless finite clauses).

Familiar non-anaphoric uses: **weather** (_**It**'s raining_), **time** (_**It**'s midnight_), **distance** (_**It**'s two blocks from here_).

**L1 note (Portuguese / Spanish):** many learners want _ele/ella/el_ for inanimates (“I bought a book; **it** is expensive” → **_it_**, not ✱**he/she** unless deliberate personification—rare in research writing).

## Possessive determiners vs possessive pronouns

- **Determiners** (must be followed by a noun): _**my** hypothesis_, _**their** results_, _**its** limitation._
- **True pronouns** (stand alone): _The idea is **mine**._ / _Those plots are **theirs**._

**Common advanced slip:** confusing **_its_** (possessive) with **_it's_** (= _it is_ / _it has_): _**its** prior_ not ✱**it's prior**.

For _’s_ on full NPs, see [Possessive _’s_](/en/possessive-s-genitive).

## Reflexives and reciprocals

**Reflexives** (_myself_, _yourself_, …) mark **co-reference** with the subject: _She **hurt herself**._ **Emphatic** use: _I **myself** doubt the assumption_ (focus, not obligatory for grammar).

Errors:

- ✱**Explain yourself the model** — English wants _Explain **the model to yourself**_ or _Explain **to yourself** how the model works_, not a reflexive where a **direct object NP** is required.
- ✱**Himself went** — reflexives do not replace **subject** pronouns in standard English.

**Reciprocals:** _each other_ (two) vs _one another_ (often taught for more than two; in practice many speakers conflate them—in formal writing, _each other_ for pairs is safe).

## Demonstratives: pointing in discourse

_**This** chapter argues…_ vs _**That** assumption was common in the 1990s._ **This** tends to mark **proximal** or **current** focus; **that** often distances or refers to **prior** material. Stringing _this_ without a recoverable antecedent weakens clarity—rename the referent (_This **instability**…_) where needed.

## Indefinite pronouns and polarity (advanced sketch)

_Composites_ (_someone_, _anyone_, _nothing_, _everybody_) carry **existence** and **polarity** nuances: _I do **not** have **anything**_ vs _I have **something**._ **Free-choice _any_** (_any student can_) differs from **polarity-sensitive** contexts—errors often come from translating Portuguese _algum_ or Spanish _algún_ one-to-one.

## _They_ as singular; generic _one_

- **Singular _they_** (unknown specific referent or non-binary inclusive usage): _If a reviewer disagrees, **they** should explain why._ Formal alternatives: generic plural _If **reviewers** disagree…_ or a pronoun-free rewrite _If a reviewer **disagrees**, **the objection should be** stated clearly._
- **Generic _one_** is formal and can sound stiff; _you_ as generic is common in guides but less ideal in some paper genres—prefer **passive** or **plural cohort**: _Researchers often **underestimate**…_

## Agreement and “notional” plurality

Collective + pronoun: _The team published **its** findings_ (US default) vs _**their** findings_ (focus on members, common in UK). **Everyone/someone** takes **singular** verb agreement in standard English: _Everyone **has** a copy_, though informal _their_ as singular antecedent resolver is established.

## _One_ and _ones_ as substitutes

**Anaphoric _one/ones_** replace a **count noun** to avoid repetition: _This **method** is simpler than **the** previous **one**._ (_one_ = _method_). Use **_ones_** for plural: _The later **trials** were noisier than the early **ones**._ Do not use ✱**one** to replace **mass** nouns—rephrase with _that_, _this approach_, or repeat the head.

## Relative pronouns (pointer)

_Who_ (people), _which_ (things, non-defining clarity), _that_ (restrictive, mixed register), _whose_ (possession). **Whom** is formal object form—_the person **whom** we contacted_ vs informal _who we contacted_. For full clause structure, pair with general [Sentence punctuation and clauses](/en/sentence-punctuation-and-clauses).

## L1 (Portuguese / Spanish): typical leaks

- **Subject omission:** Portuguese and Spanish allow null subjects; English **requires** an overt subject in finite clauses (except imperatives): not ✱**Is important to note** → _**It** is important to note_ or _**We** note_.
- **Preposition + pronoun:** _para mim_ → _**for me**_, not ✱**for I**.
- **Possessive + article:** avoid ✱**the my result** — use _**my** result_ or _**the** result **of mine**_ (rarer).

## Five-minute practice

In one paragraph of your draft, circle every pronoun. For each, (1) name the **antecedent**; (2) check **case**; (3) if _it/this/which_, ensure **one** salient referent.
