## Possession: _I’ve got a hypothesis_

In many varieties, _have got_ and _have_ overlap for **current possession**: _I have two reviewers_ ≈ _I’ve got two reviewers_. _Got_ adds **resultative** flavour: the state holds now. Avoid overusing in ultra-formal US academic drafts where bare _have_ is neutral.

## contracted forms and negation

- _I haven’t got time_ / _I don’t have time_ — both standard; regional preference varies.
- _Have you got…?_ — common in UK; _Do you have…?_ is globally safe in formal writing.

## _Have got to_ ≈ _must_ (deontic)

_We’ve got to replicate this before submission_ expresses **strong necessity** with a hint of external pressure or schedule. It is often **less formal** than _must_ in written guidelines but natural in speech.

## Advanced: not a perfect of _get_

Do not parse _I’ve got the data_ as “I have obtained” unless context forces an achievement reading; default is **stative possession**.

## Five-minute practice

Rewrite five sentences using _have got_ into US-formal _have_ and UK-neutral _have got_; mark which sounds more natural for your target journal or audience.
