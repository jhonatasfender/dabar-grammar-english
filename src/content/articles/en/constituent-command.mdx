## Why c-command matters

**C-command** (constituent command) is a tree-theoretic relation between nodes. It underpins classic accounts of **binding** (e.g., Principle A/B/C), **scope** of quantifiers, and many **movement** constraints stated in terms of locality.

Informally (for X-bar style trees): a node **A** c-commands **B** if **A**’s sister contains **B** (or **A** dominates **B**’s sister in some formulations—always verify the definition assumed in your course materials).

## Binding as a diagnostic

Pronoun–antecedent relations are sensitive to c-command:

> _John_i said he_i was tired._ (acceptable coreference under many analyses)
> _\*\*He_i said John_i was tired._ (often blocked for coreference)

Graduate seminars typically formalise this with **indices** and **binding domains**; the goal is not memorising trees but predicting **acceptability patterns** from structure.

## Movement and locality

Movement operations (however formalised—**Move α**, **Internal Merge**, etc.) are constrained so that certain dependencies cannot span illicit configurations. Many textbook examples use **islands** or **intervention** effects; c-command-based definitions of **governing categories** historically motivated parts of that landscape.

## Study strategy

Work **small**: take five sentences from a syntax problem set, draw **minimal** trees, and test c-command claims against your instructor’s definition. English examples with **embedded clauses** and **DP subjects** usually surface the interesting cases fastest.
